LVIII. ad Marcum Caelium Rufum

Caeli, Lesbia nostra, Lesbia illa.
illa Lesbia, quam Catullus unam
plus quam se atque suos amavit omnes,
nunc in quadriviis et angiportis
glubit magnanimi Remi nepotes.

LVIII. to Marcus Caelius Rufus

O Caelius, our Lesbia, that Lesbia.
That Lesbia, the one woman that Catullus
loved more than himself and all of his kin,
now in the crossroads and alleys
husks the noble descendents of Remus.


LVIIIb. ad Camerium

Non custos si fingar ille Cretum,
non Ladas ego pinnipesve Perseus,
non si Pegaseo ferar volatu,
non Rhesi niveae citaeque bigae;
adde huc plumipedas volatilesque,
ventorumque simul require cursum,
quos iunctos, Cameri, mihi dicares:
defessus tamen omnibus medullis
et multis languoribus peresus
essem te mihi, amice, quaeritando.

LVIIIb. to Camerius

Not if I were molded into that guardian of Crete,
or Ladas, or wing-footed Perseus,
not if I were borne by flying Pegasus,
or by the snow-white and swift chariot of Rhesus;
add to this place the feather-footed and winged ones,
and likewise seek the course of the winds,
which, united, Camerius, you'd dedicate to me:
but I'd be weary to the marrow
and consumed with great feebleness
with looking for you, my friend.
