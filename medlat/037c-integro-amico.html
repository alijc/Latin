Example II. On the Perfect Friend

And the father said: Example II. On the perfect friend.

A story was told to me about two merchants, one of whom was in Egypt, and the other in Babylon, and they knew each other by hearsay alone, and would send for what they needed through intermediaries. Now it happened that the Babylonian went to Egypt for business. When the Egyptian heard of his arrival he hurried to meet him and received him, rejoicing, into his home and served him in everything for eight days, as is the custom among friends, and showed him all sorts of poetry that he had in his house. At the end of eight days he fell ill. Because of the deep feeling that the host bore towards his friend, he brought in all the Egyptian doctors to examine his guest-friend. Indeed the doctors, after taking his pulse and inspecting his urine again and again, didn't recognize his illness. And because they discerned no physical illness through this, they understood it to be the suffering of love. When his host learned this he went to him and asked if there was a woman in his house that he loved. The sick man said to him: "Show me all the women of your house, and if perchance I see such a one among them, I'll point her out." Whe he heard this he showed him songstresses and handmaidens; none of whom he took a fancy to. After this he showed him all of his daughters; and he likewise altogether rebuffed and disregarded them just as before. Now the host had in his house a certain well-born maiden, whom he had long raised in order to take her as his wife; he showed her to him as well. In truth when the sick man saw her, he said: "To be away from her is death to me and to be with her is life to me." When he heard this he gave the well-born maiden to him to wed, along with everything that he would have received with her. And in addition he gave him the things that he would have given to the maiden if he had taken her in marriage. When these things were done, and he had taken her to wife, along with the things that he had received with his wife, and the business completed, he returned to his homeland.

Now it befell after these events that the Egyptian, who had lost all his possessions in several ways and become a pauper, thought to himself that he would go to Babylon to the friend that he had there, to gain his compassion. And so he took up the journey, naked and starving, and reached Babylon in the silence of a dark and stormy night. But bashfulness kept him from going to the house of his friend, for fear that perchance he would not be recognized at such a time and would be thrown out of the house. And so he entered into an ancient church so he could spend the night there. Now while the uneasy man was for a long time turning over many things in his mind, two men ran to meet each other near the church in the city, of one whom killed the other and fled without a trace. Therefore many townspeople, rushing down on account of the uproar, discovered the killing, and asking who in the world might have carried out the murder, entered the church, hoping to discover the murderer there. Indeed, they discovered the Egyptian there and, asking him who might have killed the man, heard him say, "I killed him". For he fiercely longed to end his poverty, even with his death. And so he was arrested and imprisoned. Now in the morning he's led out before the judges, and sentenced to die and taken to the cross.
