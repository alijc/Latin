Two City Slickers and a Yokel

An Arab admonished his son: "Son, if you are on the road with a companion, value him as much as yourself and make no plans to deceive him, lest you are likewise deceived, as happened to two city slickers and a country yokel."

The son said, "Father, tell me, so that our descendents might get some use from the tale."

The father said, “This was told about two city slickers and a country yokel, on the occasion of a visit to Mecca, because they were messmates until they got nearly to Mecca, and then they ran short of food so that they barely had enough flour to make one small loaf of bread.

The city slickers, when they saw this, said to each other: 'We don't have enough bread and our companion eats too much. Therefore, let us make a plan on how we can carry off part of the bread for ourselves, as is our due, so that we alone could eat it.'

Then they made a plan of this sort, that they would make a loaf of bread and bake it and sleep while it was baking and whichever of them saw marvels in his dreams, he alone would eat the loaf. They said this craftily, for they regarded the yokel as gullible to this sort of trick. And they made a loaf and put it in the fire, then lay down to sleep. But the yokel saw through their trick and pulled the half-baked bread out of the fire while his companions were sleeping and ate it and lay back down.

But one of the city slickers, as if he was frightened in his sleep, woke up and called his companion. And the other city slicker said to him: 'What's wrong?'

But he said: 'I've had a marvelous dream; for it seemed to me that two angels opened the gates of heaven and picked me up and led me before God.'

And his companion said to him: 'It is a marvel, this dream that you had. But I dreamt that I, with two angels leading me and splitting earth open, was led into hell.'

In truth, the yokel heard all this and yet feigned sleep. But the city slickers, cheated and willing to cheat, called the yokel to wake him.

Indeed, the yokel replied, cleverly and as if he were frightened: 'Who is it that's calling me?'

But they said: 'We are your companions.'

And the yokel said: 'Are you back already?'

But they countered: 'Where did we go that we needed to come back from there?'

The yokel said to them: 'Now it seemed to me that two angels took one of you and opened the gates of heaven and led him before God; then two other angels took the other and, having opened the earth, led him into hell. And, having seen this, I thought that neither of you would be ever coming back and got up and ate the bread.'“

And the father said: "Son, it happens thus to those who wish to cheat their companion, because they will be cheated by their own nature."

Then the son said: "So it happened to them, as has been said in the proverb: 'He who wants it all, loses it all.' But it's a dog's nature that they favor, and one of them wants to snatch the other's food away. But if they had striven for the nature of a camel, they'd have taken on a milder nature. For such is the nature of a camel: when food is given to many of them, none of them eats, unless all eat at the same time; and if one is so sick that it cannot eat, the others will fast until that one is removed. And those city slickers, when they wished to assume the nature of an animal, they ought to have claimed the nature of the mildest animal; and they deservedly lost the food.

Moreover, we would have wished this to have happened to them, which I have recently heard of from my teacher's account, that happened to the king's tailors on account of their apprentice Nedui, because they were clearly beaten with a club."

The father said to him: "Tell me, son, what have you heard? What befell the apprentice? For such a story will be a refreshment for the mind."

"My teacher told me that a certain king had a tailor who used to cut different clothes suitible for different occasions. Now he had apprentices, any of whom would skillfully sew what the master tailor skillfully cut out for the king. Among the apprentices was one called Nedui, who used to surpass his fellows in the art of sewing. Now there was a feast day coming up, and the king summoned his tailor and ordered costly garments to be made for him and for his household for the occasion. In order for it be done more quickly and without impediment, he added a eunuch from among the chamberlains, whose duty it was, as a guard for the tailors and asked him to watch their thieving claws and to furnish them with enough of what they needed.

Now one days the servants gave the tailor and his associates a meal of a loaf of bread, hot out of the oven, and honey with other dishes. And those who were there began to eat.

While they were dining the eunuch said to them: 'Master, why, since Nedui is not here, do you eat and not wait for him?'

The master said: 'Because he wouldn't eat honey, even if he were here.'

And they ate it up.

Then Nedui showed up and said: 'Why, since I was not here, did you eat and not save my share for me?'

And the eunuch said to him: 'Your master said that you wouldn't eat honey even if you were here.'

And he kept quiet and thought about how he could repay his master for that.

And after this, when the master was away, he told the eunuch, in private: 'Lord, my master, suffering from madness, sometimes loses his wits, and indiscriminately lashes out at bystanders and kills them.'

And the eunuch said to him: 'If I knew the hour when this happens to him, I could tie him up and reform him with whips, to keep him from acting rashly.

And Nedui said: 'When you see him looking here and there and beating the ground with his hands, rising from his seat and carrying the stool that he was sitting on in his hands, then you'll know that he's mad, and unless you provide for you and yours he will cudgel your head with a club.

The eunuch replied to this: 'Blessings upon you, for from now on I will watch out for me and mine.'
