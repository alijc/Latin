On Daily Manual Labor

Idleness is harmful for the soul, and therefore the brothers should be occupied in manual labor at set times, and in sacred readings at set times in turn. Therefore, by this arrangement we believe that time should be arranged in two ways: that is, they should go outside the first thing in the morning from Easter to the first of October and produce what's needed from the first hour to nearly the fourth. But from the fourth hour to the sixth should be free for reading. Then after rising from the table after the sixth hour they should rest in their beds in total silence, or perchance one who wishes to read may read to himself in a way that doesn't disturb anyone else. Nones should be said a bit early, in the middle of the eighth hour, then what needs doing should again be done until evening. But if the needs or the difficult straits of the place require that they themselves be employed in gathering crops in, then they should not be discouraged, because they are then truly monks, if they live by work of their own hands, like both our fathers and the apostles. Nevertheless all things should be in moderation, for the sake of the fainthearted.

From the first of October to the start of Lent, they should be given time to read until the end of the second hour; then, after Tierce has been said, they should take up their assigned work until the ninth hour. But they should all cease their labor at the first stroke of the ninth hour and make themselves ready while the bell tolls. But after dining they should be given time for reading or for psalms. During Lent they should be given time to read from morning to the end of the third hour, and up to the end of the tenth hour they should do the work that is assigned to them. During Lent they should each take a single book from the library which they should read from start to finish, and these books are to be given out at the start of Lent. But first, one or two elders should be assigned to walk though the monastery during the hours that the brothers are free to read, and they should keep a lookout for, perchance, a slothful brother who dilly-dallies with leisure or tales and is not attentive to his reading, and not only is himself useless, but even disturbs the others. If such a one -- God forbid -- was discovered, he should be rebuked once or twice; if he doesn't improve, he should come under canonical reproof, in such a way to instill fear in the rest. And a brother shouldn't associate with another brother in unsuitable hours. On the Lord's day as well, they all should be free for reading, except for those who have been assigned to varius duties. If anyone is really so heedless and idle that he won't or can't meditate or read, he should be assigned work to do to keep him from idleness. For unwell or delicate brothers, such work or crafts should be assigned so that they be neither idle nor be oppressed or driven away by heavy labor; the abbot should take their weakness into account.
