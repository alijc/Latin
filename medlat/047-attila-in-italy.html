XLVII Attila in Italy

In fact, Attila, had got an opportunity from the Visigoths' retreat and perceived a long hoped-for division among the enemy throughout the region. He was soon free from danger and shifted his army to overthrow the Romans. And with the first assault he besieged the city of Aquileia, the chief city of Venice, positioned on a sharp point or tongue of an Adriatic bay, whose walls are lapped by the Natissa River, flowing from the east from Mount Piccis.

But after the siege had been under way for many days with no breaking through their defenses, being resisted from the inside by very powerful Romans, and with his army now murmuring and desiring to withdraw, Attila was strolling around the walls and pondering whether to break camp or put it off. He notices white birds, that is, storks, that nest on roof-tops, hauling their young out the city and, country to custom, carrying them out into the country.

And, as he was a damn good observer, he had a presentiment and said to his men: "Look," he said, "the birds, who already know the future, are escaping the coming destruction and deserting the city and the citadels that are about to topple in the imminent danger. Don't think this hollow or uncertain; the coming terror has altered the habits of prescient critters."

What happened next?

He rekindled their courage for the assault on Aquileia. And they constructed seige engines and employed all sorts of catapults without delay, and entered the city, plundered it, divvied it up and laid waste to it so that hardly a trace of it remained to be seen.

From this point on, even bolder and not yet sated with the blood of the Romans, the Huns rampaged through the rest of the cities of Venice. They laid waste to Milan, the chief city of Liguria and former royal city, in like course, and routed Pavia to a similar fate. They ferociously crushed the places nearby, and nearly demolished all of Italy. And when his heart had been turned to approach Rome, his men, as the historian Priscus recounts, drew him aside, refering not to the city where there were enemies, but giving the example of Alaric, the former king of the Visigoths, and the fate of their dread king, for he had not long survived after crushing Rome, but straightway passed out of human affairs.

Therefore, being of two minds, wavering between going or staying and deliberating on this business, he delayed, until an ambassador came to him from Rome, with overtures of peace. For Pope Leo himself came to meet him in the Ambuleian field, where the Mincius River is often crossed by travelers. And he soon laid the troubled man's rage to rest and he withdrew, returning the way he had come beyond the Danube with a promise of peace, announcing and declaring before everyone a threat that he would do worse in Italy, unless Honoria, the sister of Prince Valentinianus, the daughter of Gentle Augusta, was given to him, along with a portion of the royal wealth. In fact, it was reported that this Honoria, while being held as a secluded virgin by the command of her brother, for the sake of the honor of the court, secretly sent a eunuch to summon Attila, so that he might use her patronage against her brother's power. This was an utterly shameful outrage, to secure her wanton licentiousness with evil to the public weal.
