THE LAST OF THE MEROVINGIANS

The Merovingian tribe, from which the Franks were wont to appoint their kings, up until King Hildricus, who by the order of Stephan, the pontiff of Rome, was deposed and tonsured and forced into a monastery, is thought to have endured. But it is possible that it can seem that the tribe had been ended in that king, for a long ago it had no vigor, nor anything in itself besides the plain hollow name of 'king' that it carried. For both the wealth and the power of the kingdom were held in the hands of the palace prefects, who were called the mayors of the palace, and the utmost of authority extended to them.

And nothing else was left to the king, except to sit on the throne, content with such a royal title, excessive hair, and a long beard, and to create the appearance of rule, to give audience to ambassadors coming from everywhich place, and upon their departure to deliver the answers that he had been told, or even ordered, to give, as if by his own power. When besides the useless name of 'king' and precarious wages of life, which the prefect of the court furnished to him as seemed fit, to possess anothing other than a single estate of a very small income, in which he had a house and a few servants the supplying him his needs and showing servility.

Wherever he had to go he went in a cart that was usually drawn by a yoke of oxen with a country plowman driving. In this way he was wont to travel to the palace, and in this way to the public assembly of his people, that was held every year for the advantage of the kingdom, and in this way to return home. But the administration of the kingdom and all which either at home or abroad for doing and disposing was managed by the prefect of the court.
