A Dialogue

Pippin: What is literature? Albinus: The guardian of history.

P: What is a word? A: The herald of the mind.

P: What produces a word? A: The tongue.

P: What is the tongue? A: A whip for the air.

P: What is air? A: The guardian of life.

P: What is life? A: The joy of the blessed, the sorrow of the wretched, the expectation of death.

P: What is death? A: The unavoidable outcome, the uncertain journey, the tears of the living, the support of the testament, the plunderer of man.

P: What is a man? A: A possession of death, a passing traveler, a guest of a place.

P: What is a man like? A: An apple.

P: How is a man placed? A: Like a lamp in the wind.

P: Where is he placed? A: Within six walls.

P: Which are? A: Above, below, before, behind, to the right and the left.

P: How many companions does he have? A: Four.

P: What are they? A: Heat, cold, dryness and wetness.

P: In how many ways is he changeable? A: Six.

P: What are they? A: Hunger and fullness, rest and toil, wakefulness and sleep.

P: What is sleep? A: The image of death.

P: What is the liberty of a man? A: Innocence.

What is the head? The peak of the body.

What is the body? The abode of the soul.

What is the hair? The garment of the head.

What is the beard? A distinction of gender, a dignity of age.

What is the brain? The preserver of memory.

What are the eyes? Guides for the body, vessels of light, tokens of the mind.

What are the nostrils? The conduits for scents.

What are the ears? The collectors of sounds.

What is the face? The image of the mind.

What is the mouth? The nourisher of the body.

What are the teeth? The millstones of food.

What are the lips? The doors of the mouth.

What is the throat? The devourer of food.

What are the hands? The laborers of the body.

What are the fingers? The plectrums of chords.

What is the lung? The preserver of air.

What is the heart? The receptacle of life.

What is the liver? The guardian of warmth.

What is the gall bladder? The inciter of anger.

What is the spleen? Room for laughter and joy.

What is the stomach? The digester of food.

What is the belly? The guardian of the frail.

What are the bones? The strength of the body.

What are the hips? The crossbeams of the columns.

What are the legs? The columns of the body.

What are the feet? A mobile foundation.

What is blood? The fluid of the veins, the sustenance for life.

What are the veins? Springs for flesh.

What is the sky? A turning sphere, an immense height.

What is light? The appearance of all things.

What is the day? An incentive for toil.

What is the sun? The splendor of the world, the beauty of the sky, the grace of nature, the honor of the day, the distributor of hours.

What is the moon? The eye of the night, abundant with dew, the predictor of storms.

What are the stars? A painting of the height, pilots for seamen, the ornament of the night.

What is rain? The fertilizer of the land, the producer of crops.

What is a cloud? Night in the day, toil for the eyes.

What is the wind? A disturbance of the air, the motion of waters, the dryer of the land.

What is the land? The mother of growing things, the nurse of living things, the storehouse of life, the devourer of all.

What is the sea? The road of courage, the limit of the land, a divider of regions, the inn of rivers, the source of rain, a refuge in danger, grace in delights.

What are rivers? An unfailing onrush, restoring the soil, watering the land.

What is water? Life's aid, filth's cleanser.

What is fire? Excessive heat, warmth for growing things, ripening for crops.

What is cold? A trembling of the limbs.

What is frost? The suffering of herbs, the destroyer of foliage, the fetters of the earth, a bridge over waters.

What is snow? Dry water.

What is winter? The exile of summer.

What is spring? The painter of the earth.

What is summer? The reclothing of the earth, the ripening of crops.

What is fall? The year's storehouse.

What is the year? The world's four-horse chariot.

Who guides it. Night and day, cold and heat.

Who is its charioteer? The sun and the moon.

How many palaces do they have? Twelve.

Who are the magitrates of the palaces? Aries, Taurus, Gemini, Cancer, Leo, Virgo, Libra, Scorpius, Sagittarius, Capricorn, Aquarius, Pisces.

How many days do they dwell in each one palace? The sun - 30 days and ten hours and a half. The moon - two days and eight hours plus two-thirds of an hour.

P: Teacher, I'm afraid of heights. A: Who has led you to high places? P: Curiosity. A: If you're afraid, let's climb down. I'll accompany you wherever you go.

P: If I knew what a ship was, I'd ready one for you to come to me. A: A ship is a wandering house, hospitality wherever you please, a traveler with no footsteps, a neighbor of the shore.

P: What is the shore? A: The earth's wall.

P: What is grass? A: The earth's garment.

P: What are vegetables? A: The doctors' friends, the cooks' renown.

P: What is it that makes the bitter sweet? A: Hunger.

P: What is it that doesn't make a man weary? A: Riches.

P: What is sleep to the wakeful man? A: Hope.

P: What is hope? A: A rest from toil, an uncertain event.

P: What is friendship? A: The equality of minds.

P: What is faith? A: The certainty of an unknown and amazing thing.

P: What is a wonder? A: Recently I saw a man standing still, laboring, walking, who never was. P: How can that be, unfold it to me. A: It is an image in the water. P: Why didn't I realize this for myself, as I so often saw this very man. A: Because you are a young man of good character and innate talent, I will relate some other wonders to you; try and see if you can figure them out for yourself. P: Let's do it. Still, if I say the wrong answer, correct me. A: I will do as you wish.

A certain stranger spoke with me with neither language or voice, who never was before or will be after, and whom I didn't hear, or know. P: Did a dream by chance weary you, teacher? A: Yes, my son.

Listen to another: I saw the dead beget the living, and the dead were comsumed by the breeze of the living. P: From the friction of trees, fire is born, consuming the trees. A: That is correct.

I heard the dead saying many things. P: Never well, unless they're suspended in the air. (bells) A: Right.

I saw an unquenched fire at rest in water. P: You wish to signify flint in water, I think. A: At you think, so is is.

I saw the dead sitting upon the living and upon the laughter of the dead the living died. P: Our cooks know this one. (a pot of water that boils over) A: They know it. But put your finger over your mouth, lest kids hear what this is.

I was on a hunt with other men, but if we caught anything in the hunt we carried nothing with us; what we could not catch, we carried home with us. P: This hunt was for rustics (lice). A: Yes.

I saw a something born before it could be conceived. P: You saw it and perhaps you ate it. A: I did. (an egg)

Who is who is not, and has a name and gives an answer to sound? P: Ask the papyrus/rush in the forest. (echo?)

A: I saw a stranger running with his house; he was silent but his house spoke. P: Furnish me with a net and I will enlighten you. (a fish in a stream)

A: Who is it that you cannot see except with closed eyes? P: Whoever snores shows it to you. (dream)

A: I saw a man holding eight in his hand, and from eight he took away seven, and there remained six. P: Kids in school know this one. (Medieval way of counting on fingers.)

A: Who is it that if you take head away rises up taller? P: Look at your bed and there you will find it. (a pillow)

A: There were three; one was never born and died once; another was born once and never died; the third was born once and died twice. P: The first has the same name as the earth, (Adam) the second as my God, (Elijah) the third as a poor man. (Lazarus)

A: I saw a flying female, having an iron beak, and a wooden body, and a winged tail, bearing death. P: She is the soldiers' ally. (an arrow)

A: What is a soldier? P: The state's wall, the dread of the enemy, a glorious service.

A: What is that which exists and doesn't exist? P: Nothing. A: How can it exist and not exist? P: It exists in name but not in reality.

A: What is the silent messenger? P: What I hold in my hand. A: What do you hold in your hand? P: Your letter, teacher. A: Read it happily, my son.
