The Three Friends

Because they were indeed of a single heart and soul, acting as if all three were a single man, we undertake to tell of Notker, Ratpert, and Tuotilo - as much as we've heard from our fathers,

These three, as much as they were in harmony in their vows, yet in character were, as it happens, not at all alike.

Notker was frail in body but not in mind, stuttering in voice but not in spirit, upright in divinity, patient in adversity, gentle to all, a strict enforcer of discipline among the brothers. He was somewhat timid at unexpected and unforeseen events, except for vexing demons. Indeed, he was wont to boldly set himself in opposition to them.

He was constantly praying, reading, and writing verse.

And so that I might briefly encompass all the qualities of his sanctity, he was a vessel of the Holy Spirit, filled as fully as anyone else in his time.

Tuotilo was good and useful, but in a vastly different way.

In his shoulders and all his limbs, he was just such a man as Fabius teaches us to choose as athletes. He was eloquent, his voice clear, his carving elegant and his paintings artful, his music just as his companions - but above all in all varieties of harps and pipes.

For he also taught the harp to nobles' sons, who had been selected by the abbot of the place.

A skilled envoy for far and near; effective in building and other skills, capable of expessing himself in two languages and prompt by nature, witty in earnest and in jest, to the point that our emperor Charles sometimes cursed the man who had made a man of such a nature into a monk.

But among all these things is what is before the rest, he was vigorous in choir, and tearful in secret; quite capable of creating verses and melodies; and chaste as became a disciple of Marcellus, who shut his eyes to women.

But Ratpert walked midway between the two that we've spoken of. A schoolmaster from his youth, a clear and benevolent teacher, rather stern in discipline; the brother who went outside the monastery most rarely, having two shoes a year, calling it death to leave the monastery; oftimes warning Tuotilo, who was given to travel, to beware of embraces. Painstaking in classes, he neglected most of the services and masses, saying, "We hear good masses when we teach how to celebrate them." And although he'd called freedom from punishment the greatest fault of the monastery, nevertheless he didn't go to chapter unless summoned, since, as he said, the obligation of meeting and punishing given to him was heavy enough.

Since such were these three senators of our republic they suffered constant slanders and backbiting from those given to idleness and from those who walk in levity - which is always the lot of the learned and helpful. But, since he was less wont to refute them, the sainted (as I may indeed assert) Master Notker especially suffered. Indeed, Tuotilo and Ratpert, sharper in such things and less fit for insults, were more rarely annoyed by them; but Notker, a very meek man, took the injustices to himself to heart.

And apart from these many man we wish to introduce one more, so that you might learn everything from one, however much Satan might presume in such men. What is more, since he was refectorer, he presented injuries, in the place of benefits, to whomever he intended; and to Notker more than the others. Now with Salomon busy with many things and unable to pay attention to details, the brothers' food was sometimes either withheld or tainted, and many cried out against the injustice when this happened; and it somehow appeared to him that the three of whom we speak had also been among those who had spoken.

Indeed, the prior customarily gave these three inseparables permission to meet up in the scriptorium between the night and morning services and use that hour to copy out corrections from the scriptures. But Sindolfus, knowing the hour and the meeting, one night snuck up to the glass window that Tuotilo was sitting by, and, with his ear fixed to the glass, listened for anything that he could distort and relate to the bishop. Tuotilo, a man steadfast and confident in his strength, caught sight of him and spoke to his fellows in Latin, which the hiding man didn't understand.

"He is here," he said, "and has fixed his ear to the window. Now you, Notker, since you are somewhat timid, withdraw into the church, while you Ratpert, my friend, get the brotherhood's whip that hangs in the chapter hall, and hurry back here. For, when I see you are close, I'll quickly open the window and grab him by the hair and yank him through the window towards me, and hang on to him. But you, dear heart, take courage and be strong, and rebuke him with the whip with all your strength, and put the fear of God in him."
