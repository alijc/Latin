MIRACLES AND FRAUDS

Therefore, we think it better to write a little rather than more about the miracles which divine piety mercifully reveals through the merits of our blessed father, after the dissolution of his body into salvation, to keep us from engaging in the scorn of students or the indolent or even in the danger of disbelief of the unfaithful, due to the extent of the narrative.

Nevertheless, particularly on account of certain empty-headed persons who habitually wander through sacred places in our land, rashly pretending to be blind or lame or mute or even possessed, and proclaim themselves cured on the spot in front of the altars or tombs of the saints in presence of people, rolling and beating themselves with their fists. Of course in the frenzied delight alone to secure a greater offering or gain from the common people, and thus it happens as we have often warned a blessed man to say about such things: "For they make deceits, so that it is scarcily credible to the honest.

And when they're caught out in such a blatant trick of this sort, even the true virtues of the saints are brought back into perilous uncertainty by this doubt, or surely even those who are truly cured are thought to be lying, not only by unbelievers but sometimes even by the faithful."

At any rate, this publicly happened to us with regard to some little hussy.

For in the first year of our aforesaid bishop Hezelin, in our particular festivity, that is on the assumption of Saint Mary, a certain hag unknown to us, with her head covered and her face veiled, threw herself down in front of the tomb of the blessed man and, after rolling for rather a long time there, in the manner of the insane, she finally leapt up and proclaimed herself instantly restored to sight after many years of blindness.

And this gossip was at once spread widely - the clergy and people came running, even the bishop himself came; and when they now sped to give thanks to God in public, his citizens, who had known her before and had often observed her in that sort of deceit, came, and truthfully called her a deceiver both now and also so very often previously.

And now the people rightly rose up against her and she was manhandled, but the clergy defended her due to veneration for the blessed pope. The troubled woman went away, and never reappeared after that.
