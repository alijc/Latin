The Changing World

We are compelled, albeit unwillingly, to consider the justice of God and the volatility of the world.

For look, as I said before, in the manner of the sky, that turns from east to west, we see that worldly things and the powers that be turn just like time.

We shouldn't suppose that mortal matters are found to be firm in any part of the world, but that they suffer from weakness and a sort of feverishness, as you have in Job: "They turn night into day and after the darkness I again look for the light," wherever they may turn we observe that they have no stability but have landed on sorrow and toil.

Doesn't it usually seem to you, as I said, that the character of the feverishness of the world turns and turns again?

For the feverish put their hope for rest in change and so when they turn in frequent revolutions they throw themselves this way and that.

Thus without doubt temporal power rolled down from Babylon to the Medes, from there to the Persians, after that to the Greeks, and ultimately to the Romans and under the name of Rome was transferred to the French.

Until it was as if it had fixed a firm seat, and seemed well settled, but began to sink under so many evils (as is plain to our elders) that one might deservedly say, with Job: "As I come to the day, worn out by nocturnal tortures, I await the night, but while I hold it, affected by a greater sorrow, I again long for the day to come."

In the end, when the Franks, most enterprising in arms, after greatly enlarging the borders of their kingdom and transferring the capital of the world, Rome, into their own control, had by now become horrible to all nations and seemed invincible, and were divided against themselves, not only between citizens but even between brothers, the kingdom, which was thought to have found standing and rest, as if fleeing from east to west, was about to be made desolate, just as the gospels presaged.

And so from this it is clear that you can't trust in transitory things and that anyone wanting to sustain himself can't depend on one who falters.

From which the prophet said: "Cursed is he who puts his hope in man and who makes flesh his arm.

For how will he sustain you who can not stand himself?

Or how will he strengthen you who is himself frail?"

AJC And so when the world passes, and with it its desire for worldly things, who would hesitate to shift from that desire to the living God, who is immovable and remains immutable, and to his blessed and eternal city?

Therefore, with all the kingdoms of the world diminished, and even the kingdom of the Franks, the latest to deserve to hold Rome, appearing diminished, let us who write histories to describe what has changed, with sufficient proof from this change of the kingdom, thrown back to the immutability of the heavenly kingdom, impose an end to this fifth volume.

The blessed prophet, observing the unstable and wretchedly fluctuating revolutions of the world and judging them to be most aptly compared to the sea, said: "This sea is great and spacious in its arms, there are creeping things there, for which there is no number."

Does not the world not seem to you like the sea, with stormy weather clinging to it, as if threatening destruction with the tempests' billows?

And what else are men who fight for doomed honors but the creeping things of the sea that I spoke of?

When we observe that the smaller are absorbed by the larger, and the weaker by the mightier, and that in the end those, when they have not managed to get food, are torn apart by each other.

From which came: "Great things crash against themselves."

The skilled reader will be able to arrive at all these things in the train of this history.

And so it is fitting that the citizens of Christ not plunge into the sea like creeping things, or thoughtlessly trust themselves to its treacherous gales, but should sail in the faith by ship, that is by the wood of the cross, and train their hands through love for labor in the present circumstance so that they can pass along the road of this life and arrive, untroubled, at the gate of our homeland.

Indeed, just as the kingdom of the Franks, for so long wretchedly divided, destroyed not only the Roman Empire but also part of France and Gaul along with the palace of Aachen, it remains to be told in this work.
