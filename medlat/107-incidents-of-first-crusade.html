(1099) And so, with the victory, by God the Creator, won, the commander of the Babylonian army, whom in their native tongue they call an emir, was perplexed and lamented greatly, unable to comprehend the misfortune that had happened to him.

He thought hard on the limitless troops that he had brought, the youth, selected for their strength and appearance, his outstanding arms, his soldiers' courage and, as I may say, all his horse and, what tends to bear the greatest security for the minds of weak men, that they fought in their own province, before the gates of their city, in which they had sure refuges.

But he considered the French, with an army inferior in every way, with their youth overcome long lasting hunger, with rusty swords and blackened lances, poor armor with soldiers devoid of strength, with any who seemed more distinguished than the rest numbed by bitter poverty, with their mounts exhausted with hardship of every kind; and, in short, he was amazed that the most worthless men of all, an exile band, had overcome his countless native sons; more, in fact, that through the lowliest of men they had destroyed the glory of the entire East.

It also aided our victory that, when the retreat was signaled in the enemy army and the emir in charge of Ascalon saw the Babylonian commander turn tail, he ordered that all those fleeing be kept out of his own city.

The enemy were clearly exceedingly amazed that the French had not chosen to fight before the walls of Jerusalem, as if on account of the neighboring garrison, but had advanced against themselves in a march of nearly two days.

AJC (1104) A certain soldier, whom king Baldwin himself had placed in command of the city of Tiberias, was more impudent than he ought to have been, and had behaved badly towards the king.

AJC And so the king, filled with wrath because of the man's insolence, ordered him to withdraw from the land that he controlled.

AJC And as he hurried to leave, with two knights and as many squires as an escort, he suddenly encountered a great many troops of pagans.

AJC And he, lacking confidence in his own numbers, truly respecting God in every way, cut up his shirt, which they call the under-tunic, and raised it for a banner on a spear, ordering his companions to do likewise.

AJC They did so and, raising a war-cry, spurred on their steeds and bore themselves against the enemy.

AJC Now they were terrified by this unexpected daring-do, as they assumed that these men were leading many following cohortes, and took to flight, exposing themselves to be slaughtered by these three men.

AJC Most of the enemy were killed; and they seized more plunder than they could carry.

AJC After this event he was again filled with remorse and, rendering thanks to God, prostrated himself before the king, promising that he would faithfully obey him thereafter.

AJC (1104) Once the same king was choked by a great want of money, so that he was even short the monthly wages that were owed to the soldiers; when behold, divine mercy itself marvelously provided him with a bounty.

AJC For he was by then so greatly troubled that his attendants and soldiers were on the verge of deserting him, when behold, the young men of Joppa one day were swimming into the sea not far from the shore, bathing, no indeed horseplaying, and discovered, in the very dashing together of sand and sea, sacks filled with a great weight of gold, which the Venetians had lost in a shipwreck there.

AJC And these were carried to the king, an indescribable wonder to everyone, and provided comfort to the nearly desperate king and to young Christianity.
