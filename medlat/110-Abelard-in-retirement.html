Abelard in Retirement

And so I conveyed myself to a certain lonely place in the district of Troyes, that was already familiar to me, and there, with the agreement of the bishop of the country, I constructed, on land that some men had given me, an oratory in name of the Holy Trinity, out of reeds and straw at first.

And hiding there with a cleric of mine, I could rightly recite this to the Lord: "Behold, I left in flight and remained in the wilderness."

And when scholars learned about this, they began to throng together from every direction, leaving cities and castles to dwell in solitude - to build small huts for themselves instead of great houses, to subsist on rustic vegetables and coarse bread instead of dainty fare, to lie down on straw bedding instead of soft beds, and to build tables of turf.

And indeed you'd've thought that they imitated earlier philosophers, about whom Jerome too, in his second book against Jovian, wrote these words: "The entrance of vice to the soul is via the senses, as if through windows.

The city and the citadel of the mind cannot be taken, unless a hostile army charges through its gates."

And so for these reasons many of the philosophers were impelled to leave the crowds of the cities and their surrounding gardens, where there was a well watered field and foliage of trees and the whispering of birds, the mirror of a spring, a murmuring stream and many allurements for the eye and ear, lest through luxury and an abundance of spirit their strength become soft, and their chastity defiled.

By all means, it's not often useful to see the things that used to catch you, and to put yourself to the trial of what was difficult to give up.

Nam et Pythagoraei huiusmodi frequentiam declinantes, in solitudine et desertis locis habitare consueverant. --> For even the Pythagorians, declining any sort of bustle, were accustomed to dwell in solitude and deserted places.

Even Plato himself, when he was wealthy and Diogenes trampled his cushion with his muddy feet, chose, to free him of philosophy, a building far from the city for his Academy, not only deserted but also unhealthy so that through care and attention of illnesses the assault of desire might be crushed, and his disciples would feel no other pleasure except of what they learned.

The sons of the prophets following Elisha are also said to have led such a life.

Jerome himself also, wrote about this, among other things, as if about monks of those times, to the monk Rusticus: "The sons of the prophets, those monks that we read in the Old Testament, built huts for themselves close by the flowing Jordan, and after abandoning commotions and cities, lived on barley-meal and rustic herbs.

And so our disciples, building their own huts there on the Ardusson, seemed more hermits than scholars.

But however many distinguished scholars gathered there, and however roughly they sustained life in our instruction, that much more did my rivals consider me glorious and themselves disgraced.

And when they had done everything they could against me, it pained them that everything worked towards the good for me, and so, just like Jerome, although I was hiding far from the city, removed from the marketplace, quarrels, and uproars, even so (as Quintilianus says), spite still found me.

For they said among themselves, secretly lamenting and moaning, "Look, the whole world follows after him, we accomplish nothing by attacking him but instead make him more glorious.

We strove to extinguish his name, but instead made it brighter.

Look, in cities scholars have everything needed at hand yet, thinking little of the pleasures of the city, flock to poverty in the wilderness and willingly become poor.

But then an especially intolerable poverty compelled me to instruct scholars, since I was too weak to dig and ashamed to beg.

And so, retuening to a skill that I knew, instead of working with my hands I was compelled to use my tongue.

But the scholars willingly prepared whatever I needed, as much in provisions, of course, as in clothing or tilling the fields or in the expenses of construction, so that certainly domestic care held back nothing from my studies.

Since our oratory could not hold but a small portion of them, of necessity they enlarged and improved it, building of stone and wood.

Because although it was established and afterwards dedicated in the name of the Holy Trinity, since I, an exile there and still in despair of the grace of divine consolation, enjoyed a short respite, in memory of this kindness I named it the Paraclete.
