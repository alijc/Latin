He Compares Their (I.E. Cluny's) Extravagance with the Parsimony of Monks of Old

In the beginning, when the monastic order began, who would believe that monks would turn to such idleness.

Oh how much we differ from those who became monks in the days of Anthony!

Indeed, when they visited each other in charity as the opportunity arose, they gained the bread of life from each other with such passion that they thoroughly forgot about food for the body, and often passed a whole day with their bellies fasting but not their minds.

And this was the proper order, when deference was paid to the more worthy part first; this the highest the discrimination, the more it assumed the greater it was; finally, this true charity, when the souls that Christ died for in charity were revived with such solicitude.

"But when we assemble together," if I might use the words of the apostle, "it is not to eat the lord's supper."

Indeed there is no-one who'd seek heavenly bread, no-one who'd bestow it.

Nothing about scriptures, nothing about the salvation of souls is done; but trifles and laughter and words are pushed out into the wind.

While eating, as gullets are fed from the meal, so are ears fed on rumors; and you, wholly attentive to those, know no moderation in eating.

But meanwhile dishes are set down upon dishes; and instead of meat alone, from which men abstain, great masses of fish are served in double portions.

And since you were sated with he first portion, if you touch what follows, it'll seem to you that you never tasted what came first.

Indeed, with everything prepared with precission and skill by the cooks, even after gulping down four or five courses, the first courses do not impede the later ones, nor does satiety diminish the appetite.

Of course, when the palate is seduced by novel seasonings, it bit by bit grows unused to familiar things and used to foreign sauces, and, as if still hungry, is eagerly restored to its pleasures.

Certainly, the stomach is oppressed without knowing it, but the variety takes the squeamishness away.

For we disdain food that's plain, as nature created it, while other foods are mixed together in many ways and, with natural flavors, that were dealt out by God, scorned, the gullet is provoked by certain impure flavors, without doubt the boundary of necessity is crossed but enjoyment is not yet overcome.

For who can say in how many ways eggs alone, to not speak of anything else, are tossed and turned, with such great zeal are they ruined, overturned, dissolved, hardened, or shattered; and indeed now fried, now baked, now stuffed, now with other ingredients, now served up separately.

But what are all these things for, if not solely to reflect on loathing?

Then this nature of affairs is itself seen to so that it appears from without that the appearance is no less pleasing than the taste; and when the stomach now declares itself to be full by repeated belching, nevertheless one's curiosity is still not satisfied.

But while the eyes are enticed by colors, and the palate by flavors, the unfortunate stomach, for which neither colors shine nor flavors soothe, since it is forced to receive it all, is more overwhelmed than restored.
