The Devil in a Monastery

There was, in the archbishoric of Treves, a famed monastery, called Prumia, dedicated in honor of the holy apostles Peter and Paul, founded in ancient times by Pippin, king of the Franks and Charlemagne's father, in which a strange event was reported to have occurred by all who lived there.

One morning, after the steward of this monastery entered the wine cellar, as usual, with his servant to furnish wine for the altar's offering, he found one of the casks, which he had left filled the day before, emptied right down to the peg-hole, that's commonly called the bung or spigot, and wine spread all over the floor.

And he, groaning deeply about the loss that had happened, harshly rebuked the servant who was assisting him, saying that he hadn't stoppered the cask carefully enough the previous evening and that the damage had happened due to that.

And after saying this he threatened him and ordered him not to tell anyone what had happened; fearing that if the abbot learned of this he'd insult him and deprive him of his office.

But when evening came, he entered the cellar before the brothers went to bed, and most carefully stoppered the vessels in which wine was held, closed the door, and sought his bed.

But when morning came and he entered the cellar, as he usually did, he saw another cask emptied down to the peg-hole, like the day before, with the wine drained out.
