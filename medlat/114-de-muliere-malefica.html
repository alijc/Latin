About a Wicked Woman Dragged from the Church by Demons

In these days something like this happened in England, not through a heavenly miracle but through an infernal deception; which, as I report it, the credit of the story will not falter, even though the minds of those who hear it may not believe.

I myself heard it from such a man who swore that he saw it, whom I would blush to doubt.

A woman was staying in Berkeley, accustomed to wickedness, as later revealed, not ignorant of ancient auguries, a patroness of the gullet, a mistress of wantonness, not putting a limt to disgraceful acts, who might not yet have been old, but was dancing ever so close to old age.

One day, when this woman was dining, a crow, that she kept as a pet, cawed, I know not what, more loudly than usual; and when she heard this, the lady's knife fell from her hand, and also her face turned pale; and she said with a prolonged moan, "Today, my plow has reached to the furthest furrow: today I will hear and will take great harm."

Upon this utterance a messenger of miseries entered; on being asked why he approached with such a grimace he said, "I bring word to you from that estate" (and named the place) "the death of your son and all his family from its collapse."

The woman at once lay down, wounded to her soul with this grief; and sensing that illness was creeping over her vitals, with persistent letters summoned her surviving children, a monk and a nun.

When they arrived she spoke to them in a sobbing voice: "Children, I have always been devoted to demonic arts, through some wretched fate of mine; I have been the dregs of all sins, the mistress of allurements.

Yet there was, among these evils, hope for your sanctity, which might comfort my wretched soul; desparing for myself, I was depending on you; I proposed you as champions ranged against demons, defenders facing fierce enemies.

Now therefore, since I have come near to the end of my life, and will have those enforcers in punishment whom I had as advisors in my offense, I pray you by your mother's breasts, if you have any faith, any piety, that you at least try to alleviate my torments; and indeed you won't recall the sentence that's been passed upon my soul, in truth perhaps you'll preserve my body in this way.

Sew me up in a deerskin, then lay me face up in a stone sarcophagus, and fasten the lid with lead and iron; beyond this, wind the stone in three iron chains, of great weight of course, let fifty psalms be sung by night, and the same number of masses by day, and let those check the fierce charges of the adversaries.

If I thus lie safely for three nights, on the fourth day bury your mother in the earth; yet I fear that the earth, which has so often been burdened with by my vices, will shrink from accepting and cherishing me."

It was done as she ordered, with these two pressing on with great zeal.

But, for shame! pious tears prevailed not, nor vows, nor prayers; such was the hussy's malice, such the devil's violence.

For during the first two nights, while the choir of clerics sang psalms around the corpse, several demons, breaking down the church door, closed with a huge bar, with little trouble, shattered the outer chains; the inner one, which had been more painstakingly cared for, remained intact.

On the third night, around cock-crow, the whole monastery seemed to be shifted from its foundations by the racket of arriving armies; one devil, with a more frightful face and loftier height than the others, with great force battered the doors to pieces.

The clerics were frozen in fear, "their hair stood on end and their voices stuck in their throats."

He approached the sarcophagus with an arrogant (as it seemed) movement, and, crying out her name, commanded her to rise. She responded that she could not, on account of the chains, and he said, "You'll loosen them, to your detriment"; and he at once broke the chain that had escaped the rampage of the rest, with no effort, as if he broke a rope of tow.

He also dislodged the lid of the tomb with his foot. He snatched her up by the hand and dragged her out of the church before everyone's eyes, to where a black and haughty horse appeared, neighing, before the gates, with iron hooks projecting along the length of its back; upon these the wretched woman was set, then she vanished from the eyes of the full fellowship of the onlookers.

Yet her wretched cries pleading for help were heard for nearly four miles.

No-one will judge these events as incredible if he'd read the Dialogue of St Gregory, who in his fourth book reported that a wicked man who was buried in a church was ejected out of doors by demons.

Among the Francs also more than once has been heard what I'll tell you now: that Charles Martel, a man of distinguished courage, who forced the Saracens, who'd invaded Frace, to withdraw to Spain, was buried in the church of Saint Denis when his days were over; but because he had cut the patrimony of nearly every monastery of France to pay the wages of his soldiers, he was snatched away from his tomb in plain sight by malign spirits, and to this day is nowhere to be seen.

In the end this was revealed to the bishop of Orleans and was disseminated by him among the common people.
