Arthur Slays a Giant

In the mean time, Arthur heard reports that a giant of an astounding size had arrived from the direction of Spain and had kidnapped Helene, the granddaughter of Duke Hywel, from her guardians and had fled with her to the top of mountain that is now called Mont Saint-Michel, and that her father's knights who'd followed him could do nothing against him. For whether they attacked him by sea or by land, he either crushed their ships with huge rocks or killed them with spears of various kinds; but he also captured more that he ate, half-alive.

And so in the second hour of the following night, he took the steward Kay and the cupbearer Bedivere and, unbeknownst to the others, slipped out of the tents and set out on the road towards the mountain.

Indeed, his valor was so great that he didn't bother to lead an army against such monsters, since he could both inspire his own men and be capable of destroying the monsters by himself.

And so as they came near to the mountain, they spied a burning fire on top of it, and indeed another atop a smaller mountain that stood not far from the first.

Therefore in doubt as to which of these the giant lived on, they sent Bedivere to scout out the truth of the matter.

And finding a small boat, he first sailed to the smaller mountain, which he couldn't approach any other way because it was situated in the sea.

And when he started climbing the peak, he heard a woman wailing from overhead. At first he shuddered, because he imagined that monster was nearby.

After swiftly regaining his courage, he unsheathed his sword and, climbing the height, discovered nothing except the fire that he'd seen from afar.

He also observed a freshly-made mound and an old woman beside it, weeping and wailing.

And when she saw him, she immmediately said this through her tears; "O unhappy man, what misfortune brings you to this place?

O but you'll suffer the ineffable torments of death!

I feel sorry for you, because this night such a detestable monster will devour the flower of your youth.

For nearby is that most heinous giant, of hated name, who carried the duke's granddaughter, whom I have just now buried, and me, her nurse, to this mountain.

And he will afflict on you an unheard of sort of death without delay.

What a sorrowful fate! The fair one that I raised, with dread received within her tender breast, while that wicked beast defiled her, and took her life, worthy of more lasting light.

And so, as he could no loneger defile her, who was my second soul, my second life, my second pleasing sweetness, with his filthy intercourse, for, set on fire by her the cursed act of sex, he heaped his force and violence onto me, against my will (I bear God and old age as my witness).

Flee, my dear, flee, lest if by his wont he came to unite with me, he discover you and tear you to bits in a wretched slaughter."

But he, moved as greatly as could be by human nature, calmed her with friendly words and the promised comfort of speedy aid, and returned to Arthur to reveal everything that he'd found out.

But Arthur, weeping for the lass's calamity, instructed them to permit him to attack alone; but if the necessity should arise, they should attack manfully, advancing to assist him.

They directed their path from there to the larger mountain and entrusted their horses to their squires and, with Arthur leading, climbed.

And that inhuman being was there at a fire, his mouth smeared with half-eaten pork, part of which he'd devoured, and part of which he was roasting, spitted over glowing charcoal.

Then, as he spied them, totally unexpectedly, he hastened to take up his club, which two young men could scarcely raise from the ground.

And so the king drew his sword and, holding out his shield rushed as much as quickly as he could to impede him ere he could seize his club.

But he, not unaware of the other's plan, had already seized it and struck the king on his interposed shield with such power that the sound of the blow filled the entire coastline and deafened Arthur's ears beyond measure.

Indeed, Arthur, kindling with bitter rage, with upraised sword inflicted a wound on the other's brow, that was not fatal, yet the blood flowing into his face and eyes blinded his sight.

For he'd swung his club in between and shielded his brow from a lethal blow.

But, blinded by the flowing blood, he rose up more vigorously and just like a boar rushing past the spear and onto the hunter, he rushed past the sword and unto the king and, gripping him around the middle, he forced his knees to the ground.

And so Arthur, with his courage revived, swiftly slipped away and swiftly, now here, now there, struck with his sword and did not rest until he stamped his whole sword, with a lethal blow inflicted on the head of the abomidable one, where the brain was protected by the skull.

The king, at once convulsed in laughter, ordered Bedivere to cut his head off and to give it to one of the squires to be carried to the camp to become a marvelous spectacle.

He ordered silence upon those who viewed it; but he said that he had not found another of such strength since he killed the giant Ritho, who had challenged him to battle, on Mount Aravius.

For this one had made, from the beards of the kings whom he had killed, furs for himself and had ordered Arthur to carefully flay his own beard and to send the strip of skin to him so that, just Arthur led the other kings, so too in his honor he would place it above the other beards.

Otherwise, he challenged him to battle, the one who survived would take the pelts and the beard of the conquered one.

And so, after contest began, Arthur triumphed and took the other's beard as spoils and afterwards, as he stated above, laid hands upon no-one stronger.

And so, having won the victory, as was told above, the next night they returned to his tent at dawn with the head, to which people came running in large numbers to marvel at, heaping praise on him who had liberated the country from such a gullet.

But Hyel, out of sorrow for his granddaughter, ordered them to build a basilica over her corpse on the mountain where she lay, which, its name gotten from from the lass's mount, is called the Tomb of Helen up to this day.
