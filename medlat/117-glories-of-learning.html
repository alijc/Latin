The Glories of Learning

The reward of books is most pleasant in many ways, but most especially in this one specifically since, with all the disadvantages of intervening time and space removed, they in turn furnish the presence of friends to one, and do not allow what is worthy of understanding to be destroyed or neglected.

For too, the arts would have perished, the laws vanished, the offices of faith and of all religion broken down and even the use of proper eloquence would have failed, had not divine mercy taken care for the use of books to remedy human infirmity in mortals.

The examples of our ancestors, which are incentives and encouragements to virtue, would rouse or protect nothing at all, had not the pious solicitude of scribes and diligence, the conqueror of idleness, transmitted them to future generations.

Accordingly short life, dull sense, the torpor of negligence, and useless business let us understand very little, and these same things are continually cast out and torn away from the mind by the swindler of knowledge, the ever hostile and faithless stepmother of memory, forgetfulness.

For who would know Alexanders or Caesars, who would admire the Stoics or the Peripatetics, if the records of scribes didn't mark them out?

Who would embrace and follow the footsteps of the apostles and prophets, had not the holy epistles had consecrated them to posterity?

Triumphal arches only advance illustrious men to glory when the engraved inscription teaches for what reasons and whose.

Then, in the end, the spectator recognizes the liberator of the country, the founder of peace, when the inscription proclaims the conqueror, whom our Britain gave birth to, Constantine.

For no-one was ever illuminated with constant glory except by his own writing or someone else's.

The fame is shortly the same for an ass or any emperor whatsoever, unless the memory of one or the other is extended with by service of writers.

How many and how great do you think were the kings, about whom there is never a word or thought?

Therefore nothing is more advisable to those who grasp for fame than to merit favor chiefly for letters and writings.

For extraordinary deeds are done by them in vain, to be covered by perpetual darkness, unless they are illuminated with the light of letters.

Any nugget of favor or of praise from elsewhere is dealt with in the same way exactly as if Echo, whom you hear of in stories, were to receive theatrical applause.

For it ends as quickly as it begins.

In addition to this, comfort in sorrow, restoration in labor, pleasantness in poverty, moderation in wealth and pleasures are most reliably obtained from literature.

For the soul is redeemed from sin, and is restored by some pleasant and wonderful delight, even in adversary, as the mind is made keener by reading or writing useful things.

You'll find no more pleasant or useful occupation in human affairs, unless by chance a divinely inspired devotion holds intercourse with God in prayer or, with a heart broadened through love, receives God into the mind and studies his mighty works within as if by the hand of meditation.

Trust me, because all sweetness of the world that's gathered by these exercises becomes bitter; indeed for that reason greater than he and whose sense is more complete, and the uncorrupted reasoning of a purer judgment strengthens in keenness.
