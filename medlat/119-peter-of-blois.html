A letter of Peter of Blois

TO MY DEAREST LORD AND FRIEND R., ARCHDEACON OF NANTES, P. OF BLOIS. SENDS GREETINGS AND WHATEVER IS BETTER THAN A GREETING

The day before yesterday you entrusted two of your nephews to me for instruction; one still of a boy's years, and the other fully into puberty.

But now in your letter you greatly praise and commend the character of the older one and assert that you have never found a person of a finer vein; for that reason you pray and urge that his tutor show an earlier vigilance concerning him; for you gently judge that the finishing touches are applied to him, the construction of which is due to another's effort.

But in fact the case is the opposite.

For I have better hopes for the education of the one who came to me coarse and unformed than for the other one, whose veins have already partly hardened, as if it stamped the image of a different teacher on him.

Clay and wax and other things of the sort offer themselves for the shaping and stamping, and are more easily and faithfully fashioned to the judgment of the artist if no-one previously sketched the outlines of forms in them.

Indeed, Quintilianus reported in his book "On the instruction of orators" that a certain Timothy, celebrated in skill with flutes, was accustomed to charge double fees from those whom someone else had taught.

For the work is double in these cases: once in washing out the less commendable education that had been given, and again in presenting the knowlege that produces profit and fame; for it's hard to unlearn what was learned at a younger age, since "Once a new jug has been dipped, it will hold the odor for a long time."

You declare that Willelm has a more subtle vein and keener talent because, having set the study of grammar and of authors aside, he flew to the cunning of logic; where he learned dialectics not in books, as is usually done, but in abstracts and notebooks.

The foundation of knowledge is not in such letters, and this subtlety that you extol is pernicious to many.

For Seneca said: "Nothing is more odious than subtlety when subtlety is alone."

For what does it profit those to spend their day in these things which would not profit anyone anywhere, not at home or in the army or in the marketplace or in the cloister or in the meeting house or in church, except simply in schools.

Seneca, writing to Lucilius, said, "What is sharper than an ear of grain and in what is it good for?

Such," he said, "is cleverness that frisks about in subtlety alone persists in itself with no gravity."

While lifted by his youthful levity, Icarus is borne into the sky, and plunged into the waves of the sea.

Too, while such men rashly lift themselves in the arts, they are dashed down.

Now certain men, before they're instructed in elementary disciplines, are taught to inquire about the point, the line, the surface, the quantity of the soul, fate, the inclination of nature, about chance and free will, about matter and motion, the beginning of bodies, the progress of multitudes, and the sizes of sections, what time might be, or emptiness, or place, about sameness, difference, division and the divisible and indivisible, the substance and form of the voice, the essence of universals, sunrise, and the use and limit of power, the causes of things, the flow of ocean tides, the rising of the Nile, various hidden secrets of nature, various forms of reasons, that rise in contracts or quasi contracts, torts or quasi torts, about the first beginnings of things and many other things, that examine the foundation of fuller knowledge and the more eminent meanings.

AJC The ancient practice was to train the youthful mind in the proper arts of grammar, in analogies, in barbarisms, in solecisms, in tropes and forms; in the doctrine which Donatus, Servius, Priscianus, Isidor, Bede, and Cassiodorus all expended the greatest care; because, truly, they wouldn't have done so if the foundation of knowledge could've been had without these.

AJC For even Quintilianus, who relates this doctrine and claims the tradition, exalted it with such great proclamations to publicly assert that without it, the name of knowledge could not stand.

AJC Gaius Caesar published books on analogy, knowing without this knowledge neither wisdom, in which he was most perfect, nor eloquence, in which he was most powerful, could be easily obtained by any man.

AJC M. Tullius Cicero, as is well-known from his numerous letters, diligently encourages his son, whom he loved most tenderly, in the art of grammar.

AJC And what use is it to unroll a proclamation, to learn epitomes word for word and to juggle with the craft of sophisms, to find fault with ancient writings and to condemn everything that is not found in their teachers' documents?
