A letter of Peter of Blois

TO MY DEAREST LORD AND FRIEND R., ARCHDEACON OF NANTES, P. OF BLOIS. SENDS GREETINGS AND WHATEVER IS BETTER THAN A GREETING

The day before yesterday you entrusted two of your nephews to me for instruction; one still of a boy's years, and the other fully into puberty.

But now in your letter you greatly praise and commend the character of the older one and assert that you have never found a person of a finer vein; for that reason you pray and urge that his tutor show an earlier vigilance concerning him; for you gently judge that the finishing touches are applied to him, the construction of which is due to another's effort.

But in fact the case is the opposite.

For I have better hopes for the education of the one who came to me coarse and unformed than for the other one, whose veins have already partly hardened, as if it stamped the image of a different teacher on him.

Clay and wax and other things of the sort offer themselves for the shaping and stamping, and are more easily and faithfully fashioned to the judgment of the artist if no-one previously sketched the outlines of forms in them.

Indeed, Quintilianus reported in his book "On the instruction of orators" that a certain Timothy, celebrated in skill with flutes, was accustomed to charge double fees from those whom someone else had taught.

For the work is double in these cases: once in washing out the less commendable education that had been given, and again in presenting the knowlege that produces profit and fame; for it's hard to unlearn what was learned at a younger age, since "Once a new jug has been dipped, it will hold the odor for a long time."

AJC You declare that Willelm has a finer vein and sharper talent because, having passed over the study of grammar and of authors, he flies to cunning of logic; when he didn't learn dialectics in books but, as is his wont, in abstracts and notebooks.

AJC The foundation of the knowledge of letters is not in such things, and this subtlety that you extol is pernicious to many.

AJC For Seneca said: "Nothing is more odious than subtlety when subtlety is alone."

AJC For what does it profit those to spend their day in these things which would not profit anyone anywhere, not at home or in the army or in the marketplace or in the cloister or in the meeting house or in church, if not precisely in schools.

AJC Seneca, writing to Lucilius, said, "What is sharper than an ear of grain and in what is it useful?

AJC Such is," he said, the clever trick frisking about in subtlety alone persists in no gravity in itself."

AJC As long as Icarus, lifted by his youthful levity, is borne into the sky, he is plunged into the waves of the sea.

AJC Too, as long as such men rashly lift themselves in skills, they are dashed down.

AJC Now before certain men are instructed in elementary disciplines, they are taught to inquire about the point, the line, the surface, the quantity of the soul, fate, the inclination of nature, about chance and free will, about material and motion, the beginning of bodies, the progress of multitudes, and the sizes of sections, what time might be, or emptiness, or place, about the same, the different, and the divisible and indivisible, the substance and form of the voice, the essence of universals, sunrise, and the use and limit of power, the cause of things, the flow of ocean tides, the rising of the Nile, various hidden secrets of nature, various forms of reasons, that rise in contracts or quasi contracts, torts or quasi torts, about the first beginnings of things and others than more things, that examine the foundation of fuller knowledge and the more eminent meanings.
