A letter of Peter of Blois

TO MY DEAREST LORD AND FRIEND R., ARCHDEACON OF NANTES, P. OF BLOIS. SENDS GREETINGS AND WHATEVER IS BETTER THAN A GREETING

The day before yesterday you entrusted two of your nephews to me for instruction; one still of a boy's years, and the other fully into puberty.

But now in your letter you greatly praise and commend the character of the older one and assert that you have never found a person of a finer vein; for that reason you pray and urge that his tutor show an earlier vigilance concerning him; for you gently judge that the finishing touches are applied to him, the construction of which is due to another's effort.

But in fact the case is the opposite.

For I have better hopes for the education of the one who came to me coarse and unformed than for the other one, whose veins have already partly hardened, as if it stamped the image of a different teacher on him.

Clay and wax and other things of the sort offer themselves for the shaping and stamping, and are more easily and faithfully fashioned to the judgment of the artist if no-one previously sketched the outlines of forms in them.

Indeed, Quintilianus reported in his book "On the instruction of orators" that a certain Timothy, celebrated in skill with flutes, was accustomed to charge double fees from those whom someone else had taught.

For the work is double in these cases: once in washing out the less commendable education that had been given, and again in presenting the knowlege that produces profit and fame; for it's hard to unlearn what was learned at a younger age, since "Once a new jug has been dipped, it will hold the odor for a long time."
