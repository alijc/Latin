82. THE LOTUS-EATERS

But after they had advanced a few miles from the shores of Troy, 
such a great storm rose up that no ship could not hold to its course, 
but were scattered far and wide.  But the ship on which Ulysses himself 
sailed, by the force of the storm, to noon carried within ten days
was driven to the shore of Lybia. After dropping anchor Ulysses appointed
a few of his companions to disembark, who might bring water back to the
ship and might learn the nature of that region.  And so the men who had
disembarked were ready to follow these orders.  But while they searched
for a spring, they were put in the way of some of the inhabitants and
accepted their hospitality.  But it happened that the greater part of the
nourishment of these people consisted of a certain remarkable fruit that
they called the lotus.  Which, when the Greeks had tasted, having
immediately forgotten their fatherland and companions, strengthened
themselves to abide forever in that land, in order to eat that sweet food.
