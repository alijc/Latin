========================================
Eclogue V

Menalcas:

Mopsus, since we both have met,
you to blow your polished pipe with skill, and I and to recite verses,
why don't we sit here among the elms, sprinkled with hazels?

Mopsus:

You are older; it behooves me to obey you, Menalcas,
whether we go into the shadows, tossed about by the West Winds,
or into a cave instead.  See where a vine has scattered
bunches of wild forest grapes thinly around a cave.

Menalcas:

In our mountains only Amyntas vies with you.

Mopsus:

What if he should try to surpass Phoebus in singing?
========================================
Menalcas:
Incipe, Mopse, prior, si quos aut Phyllidis ignis
aut Alconis habes laudes aut iurgia Codri;
incipe; pascentis seruabit Tityrus haedos.
Mopsus:
Immo haec in uiridi nuper quae cortice fagi
carmina descripsi et modulans alterna notaui,
experiar: tu deinde iubeto certet Amyntas.
Menalcas:
Lenta salix quantum pallenti cedit oliuae,
puniceis humilis quantum saliunca rosetis,
iudicio nostro tantum tibi cedit Amyntas.
Sed tu desine plura, puer; successimus antro.
Mopsus:
Exstinctum Nymphae crudeli funere Daphnim
flebant (uos coryli testes et flumina Nymphis),
cum complexa sui corpus miserabile nati
atque deos atque astra uocat crudelia mater.

There were none to drive the pastured cattle to cool rivers then,
Daphnis: Nothing with four feet sipped from the stream
or touched the green blades of grass.
Daphnis, the wild mountains and forests say
that even the lions of Carthage mourned your death.
Daphnis undertook to yoke Armenian tigers to a chariot,
to introduce the wild dances of Bacchus,
and to weave supple stalks with soft leaves.
As a vine is of beauty to the trees, as grapes to the vine,
bulls to the herds, and crops to the fertile fields,
so are you glory to all of yours. After the fates carried you off,
even Pales and Apollo abandoned the fields.
And the furrows to which we often entrusted abundant barley,
now produce useless darnel and barren oats;
instead of a tender violet, a purple narcissus,
a thistle with sharp spikes springs up.

Scatter leaves on the ground, bring shade to the springs,
shepherds, (Daphnis commands such things be done for him)
and build a tomb and write this poem on it:
I, Daphnis, am known in the forest from here to the stars,
the keeper of a fair herd, but myself fairer yet.

Menalcas

What a song yours is to us, divine poet!
As slumber on the grass is to the weary, as in the heat
to quench one's thirst in a flowing stream of sweet water.
You are the equal, not only on the pipe, but in the voice, of your master;
fortunate boy, you'll now be the next after him.
Nevertheless my own also just now to you in turn
I'll sing, and we'll lift Daphnis to the stars;
We'll carry Daphnis to the stars - Daphnis loved me too.

Mopsus

What could be a greater gift for me?
And the boy himself will be worthy of song, and already
some time back Stimichon praised your songs to me.

Menalcas:

Radiant Daphnis marvels at Olympus' incredible threshold;
under his feet he sees clouds and stars.
Therefore delight seizes the cheerful forests and other country estates,
and Pan and shepherds and Dryad lasses.
The wolf has no ambush in mind for the flock, nor does any snare
think of evil for the deer: good Daphnis loves peace.
The unshorn mountains throw their voices up to the stars with joy;
the very rocks and trees now sound their songs,
"Menalcas, a god, he is a god!"
May you be good, oh, and blessed to your own!  See those four altars?
Behold, two for you, Daphnis, two altars for Phoebus.
Each year, I'll set two cups of fresh frothy milk,
and two kraters of olive oil,
and most especially cheering Bacchus' feasts,
in front of the hearth, if it's cold, if harvest-time, in the shade,
I'll pour out new Ariusian wine from wine-cups.
Damoetas and Lyctius Aegon will sing for me;
Alphesiboeus will imitate the dancing Satyrs.
